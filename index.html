Stranded in Space: Mission Sedici

Mission to Alphonsus

Our rover will be traveling on the SLS mission to the moon, launching from earth into a geocentric orbit and then moving to a cislunar orbit. We will deploy from the spacecraft in cislunar orbit to the crater Alphonsus. The main focus of research is a fracture in the northeastern floor of Alphonsus crater believed to have accumulated deposits of dark volcanic material. Searching for pyroclastic deposits formed by eruptions of basaltic magma. If these deposits are correlated to outpourings of surface lava in nearby Mare Nubium then they could be billions of years old.

On The Moon

Landing (Where Specifically)

One main consideration when choosing a landing site is learning about the history of the moon. In Alphonsus there are fractures with accumulated deposits that could billions of years old. The deposits in this fracture are dark, fine-grained pyroclastic deposits which most likely came from a nearby vent located along this fracture network. Much of the moon’s surface is covered with hardened lava and scientists believed that volcanoes on the moon became “extinct” about a billion years ago. Finding places with deposits from these billion-year-old volcanoes can further create more insight into the moon and its origins. The older a landscape is on the moon the more craters it has, there are many craters within the hundred and nineteen meter wide Alphonsus.

Discovering

The Mast Camera (Mastcam), Moon Hand Lens Imager (MAHLI), and Moon Descent Imager (MARDI) are the cameras that will be installed on the MSL. They will be used to help gain a better understanding of the geography and environment of the Lunar surface, as well as provide insight into mineral and rock composition while the MSL is roving. Because of the importance in identifying the mineral composition and history of the Moon, spectrometers such as the Alpha Particle X-Ray Spectrometer (APXS), Chemistry and Camera (ChemCam), Chemistry and Mineralogy X-Ray Diffraction/X-Ray Fluorescence Instrument (CheMin), and Sample Analysis at Moon Instrument Suite (SAM) will be used to analyze compounds and molecules while relaying data that scientists will be able to use to determine whether the Moon can support life. In addition to the cameras and spectrometers, the Dynamic Albedo of Neutrons (DAN) radiation detector will be used to scan for signs of water, ice, or minerals that have molecules of hydrogen in their crystal structures.

Alpha Particle X-Ray Spectrometer (APXS) The improved APXS instrument is able to detect elemental composition more quickly and work both day and night.
Chemistry and Camera (ChemCam), this technology is an important part of telling what kind of rock is being studied.
Chemistry and Mineralogy X-Ray Diffraction/X-Ray Fluorescence Instrument (CheMin), identifies and measures the abundances of minerals on planets.
Dynamic Albedo of Neutrons (DAN) Water, whether liquid or frozen, absorbs neutrons more than the other substances. This can be used to search for subsurface ice.
